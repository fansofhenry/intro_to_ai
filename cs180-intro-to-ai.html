Our Foundation

Why This Course
Exists

AI is not neutral. It was built by specific people, with specific funding, deployed on specific bodies. This course teaches you the math and the meaning — so you can build, critique, and transform technology rather than simply consume it. We start at the root and grow outward.

🌱

From First Principles

We don't start with TensorFlow. We start with a probability, a line, a decision. You'll understand every layer before you stack them. Inspired by Amy Ko's research: reading before writing, understanding before doing.

🗺️

Choose Your Adventure

Three tracks — Novice, Builder, Architect — share core concepts and diverge on projects. You pick problems that matter to you. Like Jeff Anderson says: "You are the world's leading expert on your own learning."

✊

Liberatory Computing

Rooted in Paulo Freire, bell hooks, and Amy Ko's vision of "equitable, joyous, liberatory learning about computing." You are not an empty vessel to be filled — you are a co-creator of knowledge and a future transformer of systems.

The Learning Principles
We Work By

Rule 1: Health Comes First

Your physical, mental, and emotional health always comes before course content. If you must choose between your wellbeing and a deadline, choose your wellbeing. Come back when you're ready. Reach out to your learning partner and to me.

Rule 2: Deep Learning > Shallow Learning

We don't optimize for grades. We optimize for understanding. Building a neural net you truly understand is worth infinitely more than copy-pasting one from Stack Overflow. Find your sweet spot: the edge of your productive struggle.

Rule 3: Show Up, Show Out, Show Joy

Your presence in this classroom enriches everyone. When you're here, you teach. Peer instruction is the most powerful learning tool we have — Amy Ko's research confirms this. Bring your full self. This is a joyous space.

Rule 4: Finish Your Vegetables (Then Eat Dessert)

Complete the core learning before diving into passion projects. Vegetables = foundational AI concepts. Dessert = building the thing you actually care about. Both are required. The best projects come from students who did both.

Rule 5: Critical Consciousness Is a Technical Skill

Understanding who built this system, for whom, and what it encodes is not soft — it is the most advanced technical skill in this course. Amy Ko's research shows justice-focused CS education is empowering when paired with student trust and agency. You have both here.

The 2-Minute Question Rule

Adapted from Jeff Anderson: when you're stuck, give yourself 2 minutes. If still stuck, write a detailed question — not just "I don't get it" but "I'm working on backpropagation in example 3 and I understand steps 1-3 but I can't see why we apply the chain rule to step 4." Bring that question to class. That specificity is expertise.

Choose Your Path

Your Adventure
Starts Here

Every student engages the same core concepts each week. Then we diverge into project tracks. Tracks aren't ceilings — they're starting points. You can move up mid-semester. You can also propose your own project. The goal is to spend as much time as possible in your "sweet spot" — Jeff Anderson's zone of deep, productive struggle.

Novice Explorer

The Curious One

"No CS background required. Just bring your questions."

You'll use visual tools, Google Colab notebooks, and real community data to understand AI from the outside in. We learn to read AI systems before we write them — a principle directly from Amy Ko's research on program reading preceding writing.

  • 📋 Week 1–2: AI Audit — analyze a real system that affects your community
  • 📊 Week 3–5: Spam Detector using probability (pure Python, no libraries)
  • 🖼️ Week 6–9: Image Classifier: train on photos meaningful to you
  • 💬 Week 10–13: Chatbot: rule-based vs. machine-learned, you compare
  • 📰 Week 14–16: Fake News Detector + bias report
  • 🎯 Final: "AI That Serves My Community" — demo + written critique
Builder

The Implementer

"You know some Python. Now let's make it think."

You'll implement core algorithms from scratch in NumPy before graduating to scikit-learn. The rule: never use a library function until you've built that function yourself first. Understanding means being able to explain every multiplication.

  • 🔢 Week 1–2: Probability deep-dive — implement Bayes classifier by hand
  • 📈 Week 3–5: Linear Regression from scratch: gradient descent, no shortcuts
  • 🧠 Week 6–9: Build a 3-layer neural net with backpropagation in NumPy
  • 🔍 Week 10–12: A* search + minimax for a game you design yourself
  • 💬 Week 13–15: NLP pipeline: tokenize → TF-IDF → classify
  • 🎯 Final: Full ML pipeline applied to a social justice issue you choose
Architect

The Mathematician

"You want the derivations. All of them."

Calculus, linear algebra, probability theory — we use all of it. You'll derive backpropagation from the chain rule, understand attention mechanisms mathematically, and engage with actual AI research papers critically. Amy Ko: "understanding ML means understanding uncertainty."

  • ∂ Week 1–2: Derive the MLE estimator for Gaussian distributions
  • 📐 Week 3–5: Prove gradient descent convergence under convexity
  • 🔁 Week 6–9: Derive backprop from scratch; implement autograd
  • 👁️ Week 10–12: Implement a mini-transformer with attention from scratch
  • 🛡️ Week 13–15: Adversarial ML: FGSM attack + defense
  • 🎯 Final: Novel research question + replication study of a published paper

Portfolio + Ungrading

You Assign Your Grade.
Here's Why.

Adapted directly from Jeff Anderson's ungrading practice and grounded in research showing traditional grading harms learning. The goal is not to perform mastery for a grade — it's to build real mastery you can use in your career and community.

What You Build:
Your Learning Portfolio

Your portfolio is the evidence of your learning journey. It's not a finished product showcase — it's a process document. Include first attempts, failed experiments, revised understanding, and growth moments. Ten years from now, you should be able to open this and remember everything.

  • Concept notes for every major idea (in your own language + technical language)
  • All project code with documented thinking, not just code
  • Critical essays: who built this, for whom, and what should change?
  • Bi-weekly learning reflection updates
  • Mid-term and final learning self-evaluation
  • Evidence of peer teaching and peer feedback given
  • Your Conquering College activities (meta-learning)

Three Types of Feedback
(Adapted from Jeff Anderson)

You receive feedback from three sources, not one. The instructor is the smallest source. This mirrors professional practice in tech.

  • Self-Directed Feedback: You check your own implementations against first principles. You use the 2-minute question rule. You document your own errors and corrections.
  • Peer-to-Peer Feedback: Learning partnerships (2 people) and learning groups (4–5 people) meet weekly. You teach each other. Teaching = the deepest learning.
  • Instructor Learning Conferences: Every 2 weeks, your group presents portfolio progress. 15–20 min each. Targeted, specific corrective feedback. Not a grade — a coaching session.
  • You Assign Your Grade: At the end, you write a final self-evaluation with evidence. If you've done the work deeply and honestly, the instructor recommends an A. You earn the right to assign it.
